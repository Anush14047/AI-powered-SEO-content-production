Cost of Retrieval, Query Relevance, and Responsiveness Three core concepts to understand today’s SEO — and the foundation of our framework. Lately, I see “content chunking” making noise. But let’s clear the fog. The difference between tokenization and chunking? Tokenization → works at word boundaries or smaller semantic units. Chunking → higher-level groupings (sentences, paragraphs), mainly to optimize cost. Neither is new. We’ve had sentence and paragraph tokenization for years. And no — you don’t need a SaaS “content chunker” or a checklist. You need Algorithmic Authorship, which we’ve been practicing and teaching for over 5 years. “You won’t read a book with 80 good and 800,000 bad sentences. Search engines won’t process a website with 80 good pages and 800,000 bad ones either — because you increase the cost.” That’s why we recommend: ✅ Shorter sentences ✅ Direct information units ✅ Clear dependency trees It lets NLP systems extract information faster. In our framework, every paragraph type has a function — from definitive answers to evidence expansions — built to match how queries get augmented. Some claim: “If a paragraph is longer than 200 words, Google won’t chunk it.” Instead of listening to hype, study Steven D. Baker’s patents on passage construction limits. Sometimes, it’s just 400 characters, not 200 words. Our internal rules? 120 words for “definitive answers” Expansion comes from augmented query variations This is engineering, not guesswork. Since 2019, we’ve been preparing for the age of semantics. Those who ignored it are now chasing the hype. The reality: Engineering-minded SEOs will thrive. Editorial and attention-driven marketers will fade. Fancy dashboards ≠ real results. One more crucial point: Query Responsiveness. In our framework, this means SEO isn’t only about what sentence you write — it’s about what function you provide. Your words are only one layer. The real responsiveness comes from: HTML-level components Layout structure Visual semantics Search engines don’t just parse text — they interpret how information is delivered, organized, and made accessible. That’s why a page with the same words but poor functional design will underperform compared to one where content is aligned with query intent and supported by semantic structure. It’s not just about content — it’s about how that content functions within the ecosystem of retrieval, relevance, and responsiveness. Follow those in the trenches of query results — not those craving likes and followers.
